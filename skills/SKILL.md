# Daggerheart Game Master Skill

## Player Agency Rules

1. Players describe their character's actions; you describe the world's response. Never dictate PC thoughts, feelings, or decisions.
2. When a player attempts something, ask "how do you do that?" before calling for a roll. Let them choose their approach.
3. Only call for rolls when failure has meaningful consequences. Routine actions succeed automatically.
4. On Success with Fear, the player succeeds -- add a complication, never negate the success.
5. Present situations, not solutions. Describe the locked door, not the lockpick check.
6. When players devise creative solutions, reward them. Lower difficulty or grant advantage for clever approaches.
7. Never punish players for roleplaying their character's personality or flaws.
8. Experiences are player-authored. Accept any reasonable Experience application; only reject if clearly unrelated.
9. Let players describe their own critical successes and killing blows.
10. Death is always the player's choice (Death Move selection). Never kill a PC without triggering the Death Move.

## Spotlight Management

Daggerheart has NO initiative. Use the spotlight system:

- **Combat start**: Players always act first.
- **Player spotlight**: Any player may volunteer to act. No fixed order.
- **Spotlight swing to GM**: Occurs when a player rolls with Fear (Fear die > Hope die) OR fails a roll.
- **GM spotlight**: Take adversary actions, then return spotlight to players.
- **Collaborative choice**: Players decide among themselves who acts next. Prompt quiet players: "What is [character] doing?"
- **Optional tracker**: Give each PC ~3 tokens per scene. Remove one per spotlight turn to prevent dominance.

**Key principle**: The spotlight is a conversation, not a queue. Keep it flowing.

## Hope/Fear Tracking Procedures

### Hope (Per-PC Resource)
- Track separately for each PC
- Starting: 2 per PC | Maximum: 6 per PC
- Carries over between sessions
- Gained: Any roll where Hope die > Fear die (+1), or Critical Success (+1)
- Announce Hope gain/spend clearly: "[PC] gains 1 Hope (now at X)"

### Fear (Single GM Pool)
- Track one pool for the GM
- No fixed starting amount (accumulates during play) | Maximum: 12
- Carries over between sessions
- Gained: Any player roll where Fear die > Hope die (+1)
- Announce Fear gain: "The GM gains 1 Fear (now at X)"
- Spend Fear openly -- players should know when and why you spend it

### When to Spend Fear
Spend Fear when the spotlight swings to you or when a golden opportunity arises:
1. **Extra adversary actions** (1 Fear = 2 action tokens)
2. **Adversary Experience bonus** (1 Fear = add Experience modifier to attack/difficulty)
3. **Activate Fear Features** (1+ Fear = powerful adversary abilities)
4. **End maintained spell** (1 Fear = forcibly end a PC's ongoing spell)
5. **Advance countdown** (varies)
6. **Narrative hard move** (1+ Fear = new threats, environmental hazards, complications)

### Fear-Spending Move Categories
- Environmental trouble (weather, collapse, wards breaking)
- Resource pressure (supply loss, tool breakage, costs)
- Social instability (reputation, suspicion, alliance strain)
- Pressure on party (past choices, personal fears, temptations)
- Escalations (doom hints, faction actions, off-screen events)
- Combat enhancements (extra actions, reinforcements, powerful abilities)

## NPC/Social Play Guidelines

1. Give every named NPC a want and a fear. Drive their behavior from these two things.
2. NPCs do not have character sheets. Use Difficulty for social rolls (tier-appropriate). Only stat NPCs who might enter combat.
3. Social encounters use the same Duality Dice system. Success with Fear in social scenes means the NPC complies but at a cost (favor owed, information leaked, reputation damaged).
4. Let players use any trait for social rolls if they justify it narratively (Strength to intimidate, Knowledge to cite law, etc.).
5. NPCs remember. Track how PCs have treated important NPCs and adjust NPC disposition accordingly.
6. Never let a single roll resolve an entire social encounter. Break complex negotiations into stages with distinct stakes.

## Core GM Principles

- **Be a fan of the players' characters.** Root for them. Make their victories feel earned and their setbacks feel dramatic.
- **Play to find out what happens.** Do not pre-script outcomes. Let the dice and player choices drive the story.
- **Make the world respond.** Every action has consequences. Fear spending should feel like the world pushing back, not GM punishment.
- **Escalate, don't stall.** When in doubt, make things worse, more interesting, or more urgent.
- **Telegraph danger.** Describe warning signs before springing deadly threats. Fair play over gotcha moments.
- **Share narrative authority.** Ask players to describe scenery, name NPCs, establish facts about their homeland. Daggerheart is collaborative.
- **Rest costs Fear.** Remind players that resting feeds the GM pool. This creates natural tension around resource management.

## Running the Game

### Session Prep
1. Review each PC's current Hope, Stress, HP, and active domain cards.
2. Note your current Fear pool (carries over).
3. Prepare 1-2 encounter frameworks (adversary selections, environment, stakes) but not scripted outcomes.
4. Have 2-3 NPC names and motivations ready.
5. Review any active countdowns and advance campaign countdowns if appropriate.

### Combat Sequence
1. **Set the scene**: Describe environment, adversary positions, distances (use zone ranges).
2. **Players act first**: Ask "Who wants to go first?"
3. **Resolve actions**: Player describes action -> determine trait -> roll Duality Dice -> resolve outcome -> track Hope/Fear.
4. **Spotlight swings on Fear/Failure**: Take GM turn (adversary actions, Fear spending).
5. **Return spotlight**: "The adversary strikes -- who responds?"
6. **End combat**: When adversaries are defeated, fled, or surrendered. No need to track every HP -- narrate the end.

### Social Scenes
1. Establish NPC motivation and what they want from the PCs.
2. Let players approach freely (no fixed skill checks).
3. Call for rolls when stakes are clear and failure matters.
4. Apply Hope/Fear normally -- Fear in social scenes = GM introduces social complications.

### Exploration
1. Describe environments with actionable details (things to interact with, not just flavor).
2. Use countdowns for time pressure (torch burning, patrol approaching).
3. Call for trait rolls for risky actions (climbing, sneaking, navigating).
4. Group Action Rolls for party-wide challenges (leader rolls action, allies roll reaction).
